# Patterns — 02_patterns-and-insights/patterns/

Seven recurring signals. Each cites ≥3 evidence IDs that prove it.

---

## P1 — The real competitor is the spreadsheet, and the switch trigger is the moment it breaks under volume.
**Evidence:** E-006, E-007, E-028
Across incumbent reviews and raw seller forums, the default tool is a spreadsheet, and the recurring failure mode is the same: manual updating, no auto-refresh, drowning under volume. Market data confirms volume is rising (40.5M transactions, +3%). The buyer doesn't decide to "buy software" — they hit a breaking point. **Implication:** acquisition copy and timing should target the breaking moment, not abstract benefits.

---

## P2 — Buyers buy professional identity before they buy utility.
**Evidence:** E-001, E-002, E-003
The most emotionally charged reviews aren't about time saved — they're about *feeling like a real professional* and being *validated by gatekeepers* (gallerists). The product is an identity upgrade first, a tool second. **Implication:** lead with legitimacy and status, frame the pricing engine as "price like a pro," design the One Sheet to impress the gallerist on the other side.

---

## P3 — Pricing is the loudest unmet need in the category and its single open lane.
**Evidence:** E-009, E-010, E-011, E-023
Pricing is described as the most-asked question among artists; the prevailing belief is that it's arbitrary ("worth what someone will pay"); artists want *defensible* prices they can explain. Meanwhile the dominant incumbent (Artwork Archive) is silent on pricing and owns only inventory. **Implication:** pricing is both the deepest pain and the least-contested positioning territory. This is the moat — lead with it everywhere.

---

## P4 — Incumbents are trusted on inventory but distrusted on billing, price hikes, and empathy.
**Evidence:** E-015, E-016, E-017, E-018, E-022
The leaders' worst customer-voice failures cluster around money and care: surprise charges, refused refunds, PE-driven price increases with no new value, reports that dump you back into Excel, and "built by developers, not gallerists." **Implication:** trust mechanics (transparent billing, one-click cancel, anti-extraction give-back, workflow empathy) are a differentiation surface, not hygiene.

---

## P5 — The growing market is the low/solo segment incumbents actively price out.
**Evidence:** E-019, E-023, E-026, E-027
Artlogic explicitly "only makes sense for established artists" (£492/yr); Artwork Archive competes on cheap inventory; and the market data shows the sub-$500k segment growing double digits while the $50k–$250k middle is down 29% since 2010. The abandoned segment and the growing segment are the same segment — and it's Artelier's ICP. **Implication:** the ICP narrowing (no galleries/institutions) is validated by both competitor behavior and market structure.

---

## P6 — "All in one" is table stakes, already owned — not a differentiator.
**Evidence:** E-001, E-023, E-024
Consolidation is the most common praise for the incumbent, who owns the "affordable, intuitive, all-in-one" claim with a 4.9★/106-review wall. **Implication:** "one app for your entire art business" can be a supporting message but cannot be the *lead* — you'd be fighting an entrenched, beloved incumbent on its own claim. The lead must be pricing (P3).

---

## P7 — Transaction volume is decoupling from art value, favoring per-seat tooling on direct/social channels.
**Evidence:** E-027, E-028, E-030
Value fell while transaction count rose; the action is at lower price points; online and social (Instagram/WeChat/DM) channels are structurally elevated. **Implication:** a per-artist-seat model is insulated from art-market value swings and aligned with where selling actually happens. Instrument the DM; don't build a marketplace.

## SECOND PASS additions

## P8 — Managers need roster-wide settlement; mainstream tools ignore it, so they cobble workarounds.
**Evidence:** E-031, E-033, E-034
The manager's distinguishing pain is paying a roster correctly — per-artist commission/consignment splits, auto-calculated payouts, per-seat access. Mainstream POS ignores it (managers resort to consignment-shop tools), while the closest art-native competitor (ArtCloud, now Artlogic-owned) has built exactly this. **Implication:** roster settlement is the manager wedge AND a competitive table-stakes bar — Artelier must match the workflow and win on pricing + ownership.

## P9 — France already trusts a native deterministic pricing method that maps onto Artelier's engine.
**Evidence:** E-036, E-035, E-037
"Le calcul au point" + "la cote d'artiste" is a standardized, gallery-taught, size-and-career pricing system — the same logic Artelier's engine runs, but in the market's own vocabulary. The pricing anxiety is identical to anglophone (E-035); the formula objection is more explicit (E-037). **Implication:** in France, adoption friction is low *if* Artelier speaks the existing language instead of inventing one.

## P10 — A dated French legal forcing function is about to push the whole ICP off spreadsheets.
**Evidence:** E-038, E-039
The e-invoicing reform (receive 09/2026, emit 09/2027) legally compels every small VAT-subject artist/manager onto compliant tooling, with PDFs explicitly disqualified. **Implication:** the core "change adoption" friction is being solved by the state on Artelier's timeline — but only for tools that emit compliant structured invoices through an approved platform, which Artelier currently does not.
